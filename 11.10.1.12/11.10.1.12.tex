\section{Problem}

A line passes through $\myvec{x_1\\y_1}$ and $\myvec{h\\k}$. If the slope of the line is m, show that
\begin{align}
k-y_1 = m \brak{ h-x_1}
\end{align}

\section{Solution}

let the line joining $\vec{A} = \myvec{x_1\\y_1}$ and $\vec{B} =\myvec{h\\k}$ be $ \vec{x} = \vec{x_0} + \lambda \vec{m_1}$\\

\begin{align}
\vec{A} - \vec{B} &= \brak{\vec{x_0} + \lambda_1 \vec{m_1}} - \brak{\vec{x_0} + \lambda_2 \vec{m_1}}= \myvec{x_1\\y_1} - \myvec{h\\k}\\
\implies \brak{\lambda_1 -\lambda_2}\vec{m_1} &= \myvec{x_1 -h \\ y_1 -k}\\[1pt]
\vec{m_1} &= k\myvec{x_1 -h \\ y_1 -k}
\end{align}

slope $m$ is given by $ m = tan(\theta),\; \theta$ being angle between line and $x$ axis  i.e. $\myvec{ 1 &0}\vec{x} = 0$

\begin{align}
\cos\theta &= \frac{\vec{m_1}^T\vec{m_2}}{\|\vec{m_1}\|\|\vec{m_2}\|}\\&= \frac{x_1-h}{\sqrt{\brak{x_1-h}^2 + \brak{y_1 -k}^2}}\\
m = \tan\theta &= \frac{\sqrt{1 - cos^2\theta}}{cos\theta}\\
m &= \frac{y_1-k}{x_1-h}\\
\implies k-y_1 &= m\brak{h-x_1} 
\end{align}

Hence Proved.
